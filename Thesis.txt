ABSTRACT
This project proposes the development of an agentic AI system which is capable of reasoning, decision support and explainable suggestions. Unlike traditional agentic AI systems which rely solely on user inputs, this system features a proactive reasoning layer designed to anticipate user needs and provide actionable guidance while maintaining transparency in its reasoning process. The architecture of this system includes the following core modules: perception, memory, decision making and action which enables the agent to observe context, reason about user goals and execute relevant actions. The system demonstrates these capabilities within a focused domain of task management, integration with external APIs like Notion to perform real world actions like scheduling, updating and managing tasks. The project's primary focus is to showcase an agentic AI prototype which combines proactivity, explainability and context-aware assistance which pave the way toward a next generation intelligent system.

CHAPTER 01: INTRODUCTION
1.1 Chapter Overview
This chapter begins with the problem background, outlining the growing reliance of intelligent assistants and their limitations, followed by a problem definition and problem statement highlighting the absence of proactive, context-aware and explainable suggestions. Then it presents the research motivation, reviews existing work and identifies the research gap that this study aims to address. The chapter also highlights the contribution to the body of knowledge, outlines the research challenges, research questions, research aims and objectives that guide the work. Finally it defines the in and out scope and specifies the hardware and software requirements that are necessary for implementation.

1.2 Problem Background

1.2.1 The Paradigm Shift: From Reactive Oracles to Agentic Systems
The integration of Large Language Models (LLMs) into personal productivity workflows has fundamentally altered the Human-Computer Interaction (HCI) landscape. Systems such as OpenAI's ChatGPT, Google's Gemini, and GitHub Copilot have demonstrated "emergent abilities" in reasoning and code generation (Wei et al., 2022). However, despite their sophisticated generative capabilities, these systems remain architecturally constrained by what Russell and Norvig (2021) define as a "Simple Reflex" or "Reactive" paradigm. In this mode, the agent’s actions are selected primarily on the basis of the current percept (the immediate user prompt), ignoring the broader historical context of the user's workflow. 
This reliance on explicit user actuation creates a significant friction point. As Cao et al. (2022) observe in their empirical study of personal assistants, users are increasingly reliant on these systems for complex cognitive tasks, yet the burden of context maintenance, the cognitive load of remembering to prompt the AI with the right information, remains entirely on the human user. The AI functions as a passive 'oracle' rather than a true collaborator.  
The theoretical deficiency of this model is best understood through the lens of Distributed Artificial Intelligence (DAI). In their seminal framework, Wooldridge and Jennings (1995) define the "Weak Notion of Agency" as comprising four essential properties: autonomy, social ability, reactivity, and proactivity. They define proactivity not merely as the ability to act, but as the ability to "take the initiative" in order to satisfy design objectives. Current LLM interfaces, which wait idly for input, fail this criterion of agency. They are reactive tools, not proactive agents.  
1.2.2 The Context-Proactivity Gap
The primary obstacle to achieving true proactivity is the lack of situated context awareness. "Context" in this domain is not limited to the immediate conversation window; it encompasses the "diversified user inputs" of schedules, priorities, and historical behaviors (Yang et al., 2025).  
The relationship between context history and proactive behavior was established in the foundational work of Byun and Cheverst (2002/2004), who argued that for a system to be proactively helpful, it must "harness context" to model user behavior over time. While their research was situated in the era of Ubiquitous Computing (UbiComp) and focused on sensor data (e.g., location, device status), the core challenge they identified remains unresolved: how to utilize historical data to predict future needs without explicit instruction.  
Modern attempts to solve this, such as "ContextAgent" (Yang et al., 2025), have introduced "Open-World Sensory Perception" to capture physical context. However, a critical gap remains in capturing the Semantic Context of knowledge work—the tasks, deadlines, and project dependencies stored in tools like Notion or Jira. Without access to this structured semantic layer, an AI agent cannot reason about what the user needs to do next; it can only hallucinate or wait for direction.
1.2.3 The Trust and Explainability Deficit
The transition from reactive tools to proactive agents introduces a profound challenge regarding user trust. When a user explicitly commands an AI to "draft an email," the locus of control is clear. However, when an agent proactively intervenes—for instance, suggesting a rescheduling of a meeting based on an inferred priority—the system assumes a level of autonomy that requires justification.
Miller (2019), in his review of explanation in AI, argues that for intelligent systems to be accepted in social environments, they must provide explanations that align with human social reasoning. Current "Explainable AI" (XAI) research, as reviewed by Saarela and Podgorelec (2024), is predominantly focused on model interpretability (e.g., feature importance, attention weights) rather than the interactional justification required for productivity assistants.  
There is a paucity of research addressing the specific intersection of Proactivity and Explainability in task management. A system that makes autonomous suggestions without explaining the rationale behind them (e.g., "I suggested this task because it blocks your Friday deliverable") risks being perceived as intrusive rather than helpful (Khan & Ali, 2025).  
Thus, the problem is threefold: current systems are architecturally reactive (failing Wooldridge’s criteria for agency), they lack the integrated semantic context required for intelligent prediction (as originally framed by Byun & Cheverst), and they fail to provide the social explanations necessary to maintain trust during proactive interventions (Miller, 2019).



1.3 Problem Definition
This project proposes a development of an agentic AI system, a personal assistant designed to bridge these gaps by integrating proactivity, perception and memory into a single framework. Rather than automating user actions, the system focuses on proactive guidance which observes user context, identifies tasks and provides explainable, context-driven recommendations  that help users plan more effectively while retaining full control over decision making. The proposed system will demonstrate these capabilities within the domain of task management, integrating with external APIs such as Notion to gather contextual data and generate intelligent systems. It contributes to the ongoing evolution of intelligence systems that are not only capable of understanding user intent but also can provide personalised support to enhance the efficiency.
1.3.1 Problem Statement
Existing intelligent assistants are reactive and lack the capability to provide proactive, context-aware and explainable suggestions to support effective task management.

1.4 Research Motivation
With the growing reliance of intelligent assistance for task management, there’s a clear gap in systems which can provide proactive, context-aware and explainable guidance. Current agentic AI systems are largely reactive, meaning they execute the tasks only for the user prompt which limits their ability to enhance decision making and reduce cognitive effort. While users need to manage complex and dynamic schedules, there’s a growing need for assistants that can reason about priorities, anticipate user goals and deliver transparent, context-aware suggestions. Motivated by these challenges, this research explores the development of an agentic AI system which integrates proactivity and reasoning to support task management effectively while preserving user autonomy and trust.

1.6 Research Gap
1.6.1 Lack Of Proactive, Context-Aware Suggestion Systems For Task Management
Caption: Existing assistants are largely reactive and unable to provide proactive, context-aware suggestions tailored to users tasks and schedules.
Justification: Research shows that many AI assistants still respond only to the explicit user commands rather than anticipate user needs and act proactively. Even though the automated tools like AutoGPT can automate human tasks, it still needs to predefine a set of rules on how to react to a situation and lacks understanding of the patterns and give context-aware suggestions to the user. Thus there’s a gap in agentic AI systems that can monitor user context (task, schedules) and generate actionable suggestions before a prompt.
1.6.2 Insufficient Explainability Suggestions Made By Intelligent Assistants
Caption: Intelligent assistants often lack transparent explanation of their suggestions, reducing user trust and limiting adoption for decision support tasks.
Justification: Research shows that explainable AI systems lack the ability to understand the users that why the recommendations are made. Despite the importance of explanation in proactive systems, many existing assistance focus on automation rather than clear justifications or suggestions. This means there’s a gap in providing transparent, explainable suggestions in agentic AI systems.

This project introduces a proactive, context-aware personal assistant that addresses the limitations of existing reactive systems such as ChatGPT. By integrating perception, memory and proactive reasoning the proposed system observes user context, identifies potential needs and provides explainable, personalized suggestions to support effective task management. Unlike traditional assistants that act only upon explicit commands,this system acts as a collaborative partner while maintaining full user control. From a research perspective, this study contributes to the growing field of Agentic and explainable AI by proposing a reasoning framework that combines context-awareness, explainability and proactivity within a controllable interaction model. It advances existing research by bridging the gap between autonomous action and human-centered action where AI systems provide proactive assistance while preserving transparency and user oversight. The findings are expected to enrich ongoing discussions in Human-AI collaborations, explainable decision support systems and agentic AI architectures applicable to real world productivity applications.

1.8 Research Challenges
1.8.1 Context Understanding And Representation
Accurately understanding the user needs and representing the user context remains a major challenge. The system must process the diversified user inputs (user activities, schedules, priorities) in a way that supports meaningful reasoning without misinterpretation or data overload. Achieving this requires robust context modeling and dynamic adaptation mechanisms.
1.8.2 Explainability Of Reasoning
Translating complex internal reasoning into human understandable explanations is difficult. The challenge lies in ensuring the system can justify why a particular recommendation is made using clear language while maintaining technical accuracy.
1.8.3 Integrating With External Systems
Seamlessly integrating with third party API’s (eg - Notion) with the system to execute user tasks also gives practical difficulties. Handling API limitations, latency and inconsistent data structures further complicates the process.

1.9 Research Questions
RQ1: How can the system effectively perceive and represent user context to enable proactive assistance without direct user input?
RQ2: What reasoning framework can balance proactivity with user control to ensure that the system remains supportive rather than intrusive?
RQ3: How can explainable reasoning be incorporated to ensure that the system’s recommendations are transparent, interpretable and trusted by users?

1.10 Research Aim
The aim of this research is to evaluate a  proactive, context-aware and explainable agentic AI system that can do actionable tasks like task management while improving user decision making, reducing cognitive load and increasing trust in Human-AI collaboration.

CHAPTER 02: LITERATURE REVIEW
2.1 Chapter Overview
This chapter presents a comprehensive review of existing literature related to proactive and explainable intelligent assistant systems. It begins with the Problem Domain, exploring the evolution of intelligent assistants, the growing importance of proactivity and explainability, and the challenges faced by current systems in achieving true contextual understanding and reasoning. The Existing Work section examines notable research studies and technologies in the field, analyzing their methodologies, contributions, and limitations to identify the persisting research gap. The Benchmarking and Evaluation section then provides a comparative analysis of current AI assistants such as ChatGPT, Google Assistant, GitHub Copilot and AutoGPT using well-defined performance criteria. Collectively, this review highlights the gap between reactive and proactive systems, establishing the foundation for the development of a context-aware, proactive, and explainable AI assistant proposed in this study.

2.2 Problem Domain
2.2.1 Overview of Intelligent Personal Assistants
Intelligent personal assistants (IPAs) such as ChatGPT, Google Assistant, and GitHub Copilot have become popular in digital productivity, helping users manage tasks, schedules, and information retrieval (Cao et al., 2022). These systems primarily rely on natural language interfaces to respond to explicit user instructions, providing convenience and simplified task execution (Dey, 2010). However, their reactive nature limits the potential of AI systems to act as true collaborators capable of anticipating user needs or reasoning about context. Understanding the landscape of intelligent assistants provides a foundation for identifying their limitations and the opportunities for more advanced, agentic designs.
2.2.2 Limitations of Current Systems
Despite advancements in AI, most assistants remain reactive and offer limited personalization. They require explicit user input for task execution, which restricts efficiency in complex environments where users handle multiple concurrent tasks (Byun & Cheverst, 2018). Existing systems lack:
* Proactivity: Ability to anticipate user needs and suggest actions without explicit prompts (Lu et al., 2024).
* Context-awareness: Capacity to incorporate user context, historical behaviour, and dynamic task environments (Liao et al., 2023).
* Explainability: Providing transparent and understandable reasoning behind suggestions to maintain trust (Khan & Ali, 2025).
These limitations reduce effectiveness in scenarios requiring task prioritization, scheduling, and decision support.
2.2.3 Importance of Proactivity and Context-Awareness
Integrating proactivity and context-awareness into assistants can significantly enhance productivity. Proactive systems monitor user tasks, anticipate potential conflicts, and generate context-driven recommendations while maintaining user control (Byun & Cheverst, 2018; Lu et al., 2024). Context-aware systems consider environmental, temporal, and behavioral data to provide tailored guidance, which can reduce cognitive load and support decision-making in dynamic environments (Cao et al., 2022). Explainable suggestions further ensure that users understand and trust the AI's recommendations, promoting adoption and effective collaboration.
2.2.4 Application Areas
Intelligent personal assistants are increasingly used in productivity and task management, including:
* Personal task planning: Scheduling meetings, reminders, and deadlines.
* Collaborative work environments: Assisting with shared task tracking and coordination.
* Decision support: Providing recommendations for prioritizing tasks or identifying conflicts (Dey, 2010; Liao et al., 2023).
The need for proactive and context-aware guidance is especially critical in multi-tasking and high-load environments where human error or oversight can reduce efficiency.
2.2.5 Key Challenges
Designing proactive, context-aware, and explainable assistants involves several challenges:
1. Integration of multiple data sources: Collecting and reasoning from calendars, emails, task lists, and external APIs without overwhelming the user.
2. Balancing proactivity and user control: Avoiding intrusive suggestions while still being genuinely helpful (Byun & Cheverst, 2018).
3. Scalability and real-time performance: Ensuring timely recommendations without latency in dynamic task environments.
4. Explainability and trust: Generating suggestions that are interpretable and justifiable to the user, maintaining transparency (Khan & Ali, 2025).
2.2.6 Proposed Architecture
The proposed system integrates proactivity, context-awareness, and explainability into a single framework. While the assistant does not automate tasks, it monitors user activity, identifies relevant tasks, and generates explainable recommendations. The architecture leverages external APIs (e.g., Notion) to collect contextual data, maintains a lightweight memory of user tasks, and uses reasoning mechanisms to provide guidance that aligns with user goals. This framework addresses key gaps in current intelligent assistant research and forms the foundation for the development described in later chapters.

2.3 Existing Work
2.3.1 Proactive Context-Aware Assistants
* Yang et al. (2025) ‘ContextAgent: Context‑Aware Proactive LLM Agents with Open‑World Sensory Perceptions’. This work introduces an agent that uses extensive sensory perceptions to enable proactive assistance in open-world scenarios, and reports significantly improved accuracy in proactive predictions.

   * Contribution: Moves the field toward true proactive assistants by leveraging multi-modal context and tool-invocation.

   * Limitation: Focuses on large-scale sensory input and autonomous tool use (which goes beyond suggestion-only models) and lacks evaluation in the productivity/task domain your project targets.

         * Contribution: Provides empirical insights into proactive assistance in goal-oriented contexts.

         * Limitation: Focuses on conversational chatbot domain rather than integrated task/meeting management with explainable reasoning.

2.3.2 Explainable AI in Assistants
            * Muia & Kamiri (2024) ‘Explainable Artificial Intelligence: A Comprehensive Review of Techniques, Applications, and Emerging Trends’. This review surveys the state of XAI across domains, highlighting the importance of interpretability and transparency in AI systems.

               * Contribution: Establishes the theoretical basis for explainable suggestions, particularly relevant for human-AI collaboration.

               * Limitation: Not specific to personal assistants or proactive suggestion systems; lacks focus on context-aware task management.

                  * Senoner et al. (2024) ‘Explainable AI Improves Task Performance in Human‑AI Collaboration’. Demonstrates experimentally that assistants providing explanations lead to better human decision-making and higher trust.

                     * Contribution: Provides empirical evidence linking explainability to improved user support outcomes.

                     * Limitation: Conducted in manufacturing/medical task settings—not specifically in task/meeting-management assistants or proactive contexts.

Reviews how context (user activity, time, location) is used in recommendation systems; identifies lack of standardised context models and challenges in integrating context effectively.

                           * Contribution: Highlights key hurdles in modelling and utilising context—directly relevant to proactive assistant design.

                           * Limitation: Focuses on recommender systems rather than full assistant systems; does not address explanation or suggestion-generation in task workflows.

2.4.3 Comparative Analysis
From the benchmarking, it is evident that while systems like ChatGPT and Google Assistant provide strong contextual understanding, they remain reactive and rely heavily on user prompts. AutoGPT introduces autonomy and task execution capabilities, but often lacks explainability and user control, resulting in potential trust and safety issues.
No existing system effectively balances proactivity, explainability, and user control—a gap that this research aims to address through the design of a Proactive, Explainable, and Context-Aware AI Assistant capable of providing justifiable, context-driven task recommendations without automating user decisions.

5.2        SLEP issues and mitigation

Issue: The Digital Divide and AI Dependency. There is a risk that users may become overly reliant on automated scheduling, potentially degrading their manual organizational skills.

Mitigation: To address the ethical risk of misinformation, the UI includes a "Validation Layer." Users must explicitly confirm an action before it is written to their Notion database, preventing the AI from making unverified changes.
	Professional
Issue: Professional Competence and Code Integrity. Providing a tool that modifies a user's primary workspace requires a high standard of software reliability to prevent data corruption.
Mitigation: Adhering to the BCS Code of Conduct, rigorous unit testing was performed on the Notion CRUD modules.

7.2 Technology Selection

7.2.1 LLM Selection
Qwen3-32B was selected as the language model for intent classification and task prioritization due to its extended reasoning capability through chain-of-thought thinking, which is critical for reliably detecting ambiguous user intent (UNCLEAR classification) in destructive operations such as task deletion. Empirical testing demonstrated that smaller models without inherent reasoning (e.g., Llama 3.1 8B) exhibited overconfident task matching, executing delete operations on partially-matching tasks rather than correctly flagging them as ambiguous, a behaviour unacceptable in a task management context. Qwen3-32B's internal reasoning process acts as a confidence calibration mechanism, making it well-suited for an agent that must balance responsiveness with data integrity.
